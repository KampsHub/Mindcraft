# Exercise design

The pedagogical ruleset for every exercise in Mindcraft. Every exercise must complete the full arc — Learn → Recognize → Practice — and produce an artifact, not just a reflection.

## The 11-item checklist (run on every exercise)

1. What is the **specific learning point**? State it in one sentence. Not "learn about Gottman" — that's a topic. "Recognize which horseman shows up in your conflicts and know the specific replacement behavior" — that's a learning point.
2. Does `whyThis` **teach** the framework, not just mention it?
3. Does the primitive **deliver the point**, or just create busywork?
4. Does the instruction describe the **outcome**, not just the mechanic?
5. Does `whyThis` use the user's **actual words** from the scenario?
6. Does `prePopulated` data come from the **scenario**, not a template?
7. Does the exercise **end with the user having something new** (reframe, skill, pattern, plan)?
8. If the exercise promises something, does the **primitive actually deliver it**?
9. For named frameworks (Gottman, NVC, IFS, ACT, DBT, Kegan & Lahey, Polyvagal), does it **teach the framework**?
10. Were exercises designed **back-to-front**?
11. **Did you render the exercise in the preview and look at it** before writing content? Never write in isolation from the primitive's rendered state.

## Core principles (most-violated)

- **Design back-to-front**: payoff first, then scenario
- **One exercise = one concept** — don't combine somatic + cognitive
- **Never give someone overwhelmed a blank page** — always seed with relatable examples
- **Exercises must produce artifacts, not just reflections**
- **Primitive IS the pedagogy** — don't retrofit a framework onto a mismatched primitive
- **Use established coaching tools** — Chamine saboteurs, Gottman antidotes, validated values lists — not improvised grids
- **Pull forward previous exercise data** when an exercise references earlier work

## Primitives to watch

- CardSort, ForceField, StakeholderMap, WheelChart — most likely to have placeholder data that contradicts the scenario
- EmotionWheel — only for affect labeling, NOT for frameworks about entities/parts/roles
- Guided (text-only) — vulnerable to becoming "reflect on X" without giving a framework to reflect *with*
- Any primitive where step 1 is categorize/rate/map — check that step 2 (so what? now what?) is actually built in
