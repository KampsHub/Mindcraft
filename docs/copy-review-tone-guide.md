# Mindcraft Copy Review & Tone Guide

## Who is landing on this page?

Someone at a breaking point in their career. They were laid off, put on a PIP, or started a new role that's drowning them in doubt. They feel:

- **Embarrassed** — they identified with their job, and it's gone or under threat
- **Overwhelmed** — their mind won't stop spinning; they can't think clearly
- **Ashamed** — society tells them to "push through," network harder, stay positive
- **Lost** — they don't know who they are without the title or the role

They are NOT in problem-solving mode. They are not optimizing. They are barely holding it together. Per the data, these events rank among life's most stressful.

---

## What we are NOT doing

- **We are not selling a product to people in pain.** We are offering structure when everything feels like chaos.
- **We are not profiting off a crisis.** We built this because the founder went through it and wished it existed.
- **We are not promising to fix them.** They aren't broken. They're overwhelmed. We help them see clearly again.
- **We are not a productivity tool.** We don't help them "crush their goals." We help them slow down enough to figure out what's actually going on.

---

## What we ARE offering

A structured way to think through and deal with a major career blowback, using exercises grounded in:

- **Neuroscience** — how the brain processes threat, loss, and identity disruption
- **Coaching** — the frameworks certified coaches use in real sessions
- **Performance psychology** — evidence-based methods for functioning under pressure
- **Relationship management** — Gottman, IFS, and relational tools for how crisis affects how we show up with others

The program is two things at once:

1. **A structured arc** based on common needs and reactions in each situation (layoff, PIP, new role) — what coaching and research say most people need
2. **A personal mirror** that detects individual patterns and blind spots that may be obstacles to moving forward — things that are hard to see when you're overwhelmed

And when the screen isn't enough: **human coaches are available** who can see the person's program data and already know their story.

## Tone rules

### Lead with acknowledgment, not features
The person needs to feel seen before they can process information about a product. Every section should earn the right to talk about what Mindcraft does by first acknowledging what the person is going through.

**Bad:** "250+ evidence-based frameworks matched to your journal."
**Good:** "Every exercise is chosen for you — from frameworks used in real coaching rooms — based on what showed up in your journal that day."

### Never use commanding language
Words like "do the work," "the real work starts," and "the work continues" sound like a boot camp instructor. The person can barely get out of bed. Frame activities as support, not obligation.

**Bad:** "Do the work."
**Good:** "Today's exercise."

**Bad:** "The work continues."
**Good:** "Welcome back."

### Describe frameworks by what they address, not by name
The target audience doesn't know what IFS, ACT, or polyvagal theory are. Listing acronyms is credentialing for other coaches. Connect with the person by describing what the tools do.

**Bad:** "IFS, ACT, Gottman, polyvagal, performance psychology"
**Good:** "exercises for your thoughts, your body, and your relationships"

Exception: Name frameworks once in the FAQ or trust section where someone has scrolled far enough to be evaluating credibility. There, pair each name with the problem it solves: "parts work for the inner critic, defusion for the spiraling thoughts, somatic grounding for when the body won't settle."

### Use "250+" exactly once
The framework count appears credible the first time. By the fourth mention it reads as a marketing talking point. Use it once, in a credibility context (FAQ or trust section). Everywhere else, describe what the frameworks do.

### Don't apply social pressure
Phrases like "most people keep going" imply that leaving is the wrong choice. For someone already insecure, this triggers fear of making the wrong decision. Use "some" instead of "most" when describing optional continuations.

### Don't ask if they're "ready"
"Ready to start?" pressures someone who may not feel ready for anything. They're here because they're desperate, not because they're motivated. Frame calls to action as offering support, not demanding commitment.

**Bad:** "Ready to start?"
**Good:** "You don't have to figure this out alone."

### Remove blaming language
When describing patterns, avoid language that implies failure or weakness. The person's coping mechanisms had reasons to form. Avoidance is protective, not lazy.

**Bad:** "the loops you keep falling into, the things you've been avoiding"
**Good:** "the loops that keep running, the things that are hard to look at"

### Don't self-congratulate
Phrases like "the daily program holds a lot" or "the program does a lot" are the product patting itself on the back. The reader doesn't need to hear how good the program is before being told they can talk to a human.

**Bad:** "The daily program holds a lot — but some moments need a human voice."
**Good:** "Some moments need a human voice."

### Earned honesty builds trust
When answering "does this actually work?" — lead with honesty. "It depends on whether you show up" is more respectful than an unqualified yes. It also reinforces that this requires engagement, not passive consumption.

## Section-by-section guide

### Hero
Acknowledge the emotional reality first. Don't pivot to product-speak in the subheadline. No jargon. The subheadline earns the right to mention structure by first naming the crisis.

### Reframe block
This is the most important copy on the page. It names the toxic advice they're getting, validates the magnitude of what they're going through, and offers a different path. Keep the last line in the reader's world — don't pivot to "real structure" (a selling point). End with "work through it — not around it."

### Program cards
Lead with the feeling, not the fix. "The panic is louder than the feedback." "The job is gone. The feelings aren't." These are the strongest copy on the site — they speak to the inner experience, not the product benefit.

### How it Works
Position the dual nature: structured program (common needs) + personal mirror (pattern detection). Include the human coach. Use cards, not a flat list — this is the key section.

### Trust section
The reader is sharing vulnerable things. Lead with privacy and warmth. Don't brag about framework count here — say "every exercise is cited and explained."

### Who built this
Lead with the pain, not the building. "The grief, the shame, the identity crisis — it stayed with me for years" is the most credible line. The product is secondary to the lived experience.

### Takeaways
"Not a fix. A shift in how you see things." Manage expectations without being defeatist. Show the reader that the bar isn't "fixed" — it's "clearer."

### FAQ
Include "I'm barely holding it together. Do I have time for this?" — it shows we know who's reading. Be honest in all answers. Don't oversell.

### Final CTA
"You don't have to white-knuckle through this." No pressure. No urgency. Just an open door.

### Internal pages (post-signup)
The tone shift matters even more here — the person has committed. Greet them warmly ("Welcome back"), not with demands ("The work continues"). Exercises are offered, not commanded.

## Differentiation summary

Mindcraft is NOT:
- A meditation app (Headspace/Calm) — those are for general wellbeing
- A chatbot (ChatGPT) — that responds to one message at a time with no memory
- A journaling app (Rosebud/Reflectly) — that gives you a blank page and maybe a mood tracker
- Therapy — we're not licensed clinicians processing the past
- A generic self-help course — our programs are built for specific crisis moments

Mindcraft IS:
- A structured 30-day coaching program for specific career crises
- Built on 250+ frameworks from neuroscience, coaching, performance psychology, and relationship management
- Both a structured program (what research says most people need) AND a personal mirror (detecting individual patterns and blind spots)
- Pattern detection across entries over time — not single-session responses
- Daily adaptation based on what's emerging in the journal
- Human coaches available who can see program data and already know the person's story
- Built by a certified coach who went through it herself and wished this had existed
