# PARACHUTE — Layoff Program Coaching Plan Exercises

> Program-specific exercises for the 30-day PARACHUTE program. These are the **required coaching plan exercises** tied to specific days in the program arc. They are unique to the layoff program and not shared with other programs.

---

## Week 1: GROUND — What Actually Happened

### Seven Disruptions Inventory

**Day:** 1 | **Duration:** 5 min | **Onboarding:** Yes

Map all seven disruptions on a scale of 1–10. This is your baseline.

**How to run it:**
1. Rate each disruption 1–10 (10 = most intense):
   - Income and Financial Security
   - Routine and Structure
   - Identity
   - Social Belonging
   - Sense of Competence
   - Future Uncertainty
   - Skill Confidence
2. Financial runway estimate: How many months can you sustain current spending?
3. Support system: Who knows? Who have you told? Who do you lean on?
4. What have you tried so far to process this?
5. Honest baseline. No reframing. Just where you are.

**Purpose:** Extends pre-start intake. Creates measurable baseline for Day 24 reassessment.

---

### Timeline Mapping

**Day:** 2 | **Duration:** 5 min | **Onboarding:** Yes

Map the last 6–12 months. Not just events — the emotional subtext underneath.

**How to run it:**
1. Draw a timeline: last 6–12 months
2. Mark key events: first warning signs, conversations, decisions, the day itself
3. For each event: what were you feeling? What did you tell yourself?
4. Relationship dynamics: who was involved? What shifted between you and key people?
5. Decision points: where did you have agency? Where didn't you?

**Purpose:** Captures narrative context for pattern detection. Analytical clients produce clean timelines — prompts push toward emotional subtext.

---

### Somatic Mapping + Family Patterns

**Day:** 3 | **Duration:** 5 min | **Onboarding:** Yes

Where does the loss live in your body? What did your family teach you about work and worth?

**How to run it:**
1. Body scan: where is tension, numbness, activation related to the job loss?
2. Map it: chest, stomach, shoulders, jaw, hands — name what you find
3. Family patterns:
   - What did your family believe about people who lose their jobs?
   - What was the message about professional achievement?
   - What happened when someone in your family struggled professionally?
   - What was rewarded? What was punished?
4. Connect: how do these family teachings show up in how you're processing this?

**Purpose:** Feeds Week 2 identity and belief work. After Day 3: system generates 6 goals.

---

### Saboteur Identification

**Day:** 4 | **Duration:** 5 min

Identify the top 2–3 inner critic patterns that are loudest right now.

**How to run it:**
1. What are the top 2–3 critical voices about your job loss?
2. For each saboteur:
   - What does it say? (Exact words)
   - When is it loudest? (Time of day, triggers, situations)
   - What is it trying to protect you from?
3. Give each a name that creates distance (The Prosecutor, The Perfectionist, etc.)
4. Notice: these voices predate this job. When did you first hear them?

**Purpose:** Cornerstone exercise. If Enneagram available: cross-references saboteur with type structure.

---

### Financial Ground Truth

**Day:** 5 | **Duration:** 5 min

Separate financial facts from financial fear.

**How to run it:**
1. Burn rate: monthly essential expenses (actual numbers)
2. Runway: months of savings at current burn rate
3. Insurance: status and timeline
4. Severance: amount and timeline
5. Three actions in your control right now
6. Three fears that are projections, not facts
7. The feeling underneath the financial anxiety — what is money representing?

**Purpose:** Concrete. Earns trust for less concrete work ahead.

---

### Minimal Structure Design

**Day:** 6 | **Duration:** 3 min

Design the smallest structure that would help — not a productivity system.

**How to run it:**
1. Choose three daily anchors:
   - One physical (walk, workout, cooking)
   - One reflective (this program)
   - One connective (call someone, text, be with people)
2. When? Assign rough times. Not rigid.
3. This is a floor, not a ceiling. It prevents dissolving into anxiety.
4. What old routine do you miss? What routine are you free of?

**Purpose:** Structure is not productivity. It is a container against dissolving into anxiety.

---

### Acceptance Curve Placement

**Day:** 7 | **Duration:** 3 min

Where are you? Not where you should be.

**How to run it:**
1. Place yourself on the acceptance spectrum:
   - Denial / Shock
   - Anger / Frustration
   - Bargaining / Replaying
   - Sadness / Grief
   - Acceptance / Integration
2. What keeps pulling you backward?
3. What creates forward movement?
4. What shifted since Day 1?
5. No pressure to be further along. Placement is awareness, not judgment.

**Purpose:** End of Week 1 check-in. Healthy grief oscillates — getting stuck in one position is the risk.

## Week 2: DIG — What You Believe and Where It Came From

### Role Identity Transition

**Day:** 8 | **Duration:** 5 min

Who were you in that role? Who are you without it?

**How to run it:**
1. "In my last role I was known as ___"
2. "My status came from ___"
3. Which parts of you mourn that identity?
4. Which parts are relieved?
5. "The competency that matters beyond any role is ___"
6. "Emerging: what I'm noticing about myself without the title is ___"

**Source:** Herminia Ibarra (Working Identity) + IFS parts work (Schwartz)

**Purpose:** Identity is not dissolved by job loss — it is revealed. The role was a container. What's underneath?

---

### Belonging Inventory

**Day:** 9 | **Duration:** 3 min

Where do you still belong?

**How to run it:**
1. List: groups, people, places where you experience belonging
2. Separate work belonging from life belonging
3. Where are the gaps? What communities did you lose access to?
4. One place you could reconnect this week
5. Social connection regulates the nervous system — isolation after job loss is dysregulating

**Purpose:** Belonging is a nervous system need, not a luxury.

---

### Belief Inventory

**Day:** 10 | **Duration:** 5 min

Map the beliefs you carry about yourself as a professional.

**How to run it:**
1. List 5–10 beliefs about yourself as a professional
2. For each: where did it originate? (Family, first boss, specific experience)
3. Which existed before this job? Which are new since the loss?
4. Which serve you? Which are loud but not helpful?
5. Star the ones that feel most like facts. Those are the ones to examine.

**Purpose:** Beliefs under stress encode disproportionately. They feel like truth because they were written during threat.

---

### Family Patterns: Work and Worth

**Day:** 11 | **Duration:** 5 min

The achievement equation you inherited.

**How to run it:**
1. What did your family believe about unemployed people?
2. What company reward systems did you internalize as identity?
3. When did work = worth get installed? Specific memory if possible.
4. Separate: what you believe when the inherited voice is quiet vs. when it's loud
5. Self-worth attached to title — be precise about what's attached to what

**Source:** Family systems theory + identity development

**Purpose:** Heaviest day for many. If Enneagram Type 3 or 8 wing: system notes identity-achievement fusion.

---

### Saboteur Patterns in Action

**Day:** 12 | **Duration:** 3 min

Track your saboteurs in the wild.

**How to run it:**
1. For each saboteur identified on Day 4:
   - When did it show up this week?
   - What specific situation triggered it?
   - What did it drive you to do (or avoid)?
2. Practice the gap: the space between noticing the saboteur and being run by it
3. The saboteur as a colleague with bad data — how would you respond to that colleague?

**Purpose:** The gap between noticing a pattern and being run by it is where growth happens.

---

### Values Excavation Part 1

**Day:** 13 | **Duration:** 5 min

Find your values in your actual experience — not from a menu.

**How to run it:**
1. Three career moments when you felt fully yourself — aligned, energized, real
2. For each moment:
   - What was happening?
   - Who was there?
   - What made it feel right?
3. Extract the values present in those moments
4. Your values exist in your history. They are not aspirational. They are patterns.

**Purpose:** Values from experience are stickier than values from lists.

---

### Values Stack Ranking

**Day:** 14 | **Duration:** 5 min

Force the tradeoffs.

**How to run it:**
1. Take the values from Day 13 and any others that surfaced
2. Forced rank: only three can be non-negotiable
3. When two conflict, which wins? (Autonomy vs. security? Impact vs. stability?)
4. Look at your last job through these three: which were honored? Which violated?
5. Uncomfortable by design. The difficulty IS the exercise.

**Purpose:** End of Week 2. Values you can't rank are values you won't use as a compass.

## Week 3: BUILD — Competence, Narrative, and Forward Movement

### Values Pressure Test

**Day:** 15 | **Duration:** 5 min

Stress-test your ranked values from Day 14 against realistic dilemmas.

**How to run it:**
Pull the user's top 3 ranked values from Day 14. Generate 5 forced-choice scenarios where exactly two of their values are in direct tension. Each scenario presents a realistic career dilemma (not abstract) and forces a choice between Option A (honoring Value X) and Option B (honoring Value Y).

Example scenarios (if user's top values are Autonomy, Impact, Security):
- Scenario 1 — Autonomy vs. Security: "A stable corporate role offers a 2-year contract with great benefits, but you'd report to a micromanager with zero flexibility."
- Scenario 2 — Impact vs. Security: "A nonprofit doing work you believe in deeply offers you a role — at 30% less than market rate."
- Scenario 3 — Autonomy vs. Impact: "You can freelance on your own terms, but the most meaningful project requires joining a team full-time."

After each choice: "Did that feel easy or did it cost something? The ones that cost something are the most informative."

After all 5: "Look at the pattern — are your choices consistent with the ranking from Day 14, or did pressure rearrange them?"

**Primitive:** ForcedChoiceMatrix — each row is a scenario, two columns for the two values in tension, user selects one.

**Purpose:** Values only predict behavior when activated under realistic conditions, not ranked in the abstract (Schwartz, Hebrew University of Jerusalem). The gap between stated and pressure-tested values is some of the most useful information you can have.

---

### Structural Perspective

**Day:** 16 | **Duration:** 5 min

Separate what happened TO you from what happened BECAUSE of you.

**How to run it:**
Use a SplitAnnotator with three columns: "What happened" | "Tag" | "What this means for me." The user lists specific factors that contributed to their job loss, then tags each row as 'structural' (outside your control), 'personal' (within your control), or 'mixed.'

Pre-populate with examples that are clearly differentiated:
- "Company laid off 15% of workforce" → structural → "This wasn't about my performance"
- "My role was eliminated when the team was restructured" → structural → "The work I did still mattered"
- "AI tools replaced part of my function" → structural → "I need to understand where my skills still apply"
- "I didn't adapt quickly enough to new tooling" → personal → "I can close this gap"
- "I avoided difficult conversations with my manager" → personal → "I want to handle this differently next time"
- "Budget cuts hit my department hardest" → mixed → "The cuts were structural but I could have been more visible"

Layout: single-column list (not left-right, which implies causation). Each row has the event, the tag selector, and a text field for what it means.

After tagging all rows: "Count the structural vs. personal tags. Most people carry far more personal shame than the facts warrant. What shifts when you see the actual ratio?"

**Purpose:** Locating, not minimizing. Your pain is real AND the system produced much of it. Separating what's yours to own from what's not is the prerequisite for effective forward action.

### Evidence Collection

**Day:** 17 | **Duration:** 5 min

Build the counter-record your negativity bias keeps forgetting.

**How to run it:**
Use a SplitAnnotator with two columns: "What I Did" | "The Evidence." In the left column, the user writes something concrete they did well in their career. In the right column, they note the impact it had and who noticed: a result, a comment from a colleague, a metric, a problem solved. Each row also has tags the user selects from: 'leadership' (people and team moments), 'technical' (skills and craft), 'impact' (results and outcomes).

Pre-populate with strength-and-leadership-focused examples (not project deliverables):
- "Kept the team calm during a crisis" → "Shipped on time despite losing two people; director said I held it together"
- "Mentored two junior engineers" → "Both got promoted within a year; one reached out after my layoff to say thanks"
- "Spoke up when everyone else stayed silent" → "Changed the direction of the project; PM told me privately I saved them months"
- "Handled the client escalation calmly" → "Client renewed their contract; manager said I 'saved the relationship'"
- "Built trust across departments" → "Got invited to cross-functional meetings others couldn't get into"

Aim for at least 8 entries. Include things nobody thanked you for and things that came so naturally you dismissed them. When you're done, read the full list. This is your counter-record — the evidence your negativity bias keeps forgetting.

**Purpose:** This list feeds Day 21's skill awareness work. The user needs concrete evidence of what they're good at before they can map their capabilities.

### Narrative Construction Part 1

**Day:** 18 | **Duration:** 5 min

Three versions of the story.

**How to run it:**
1. Write the acquaintance version — what you say at a party
2. Write the close friend version — what you say after two glasses of wine
3. Write the 3am version — what you say to yourself at night
4. Compare. Which is closest to true?
5. Which version are you most attached to? Why?

### Skill Discovery

**Day:** 19 | **Duration:** 5 min

Find the capabilities hidden inside your evidence.

**How to run it:**
Pull the user's entries from Day 17's Evidence Collection. For each entry, extract the underlying capability — not the project, but the skill it demonstrates.

Use a SplitAnnotator with three columns: "Evidence (from Day 17)" | "The Skill Underneath" | "Where Else This Shows Up."

Pre-populate by transforming Day 17 examples:
- "Kept the team calm during a crisis" → "Steadiness under pressure" → "Also showed up when..."
- "Mentored two junior engineers" → "Developing others" → ""
- "Spoke up when everyone else stayed silent" → "Courageous advocacy" → ""
- "Handled the client escalation calmly" → "De-escalation and repair" → ""
- "Built trust across departments" → "Cross-boundary influence" → ""

The skill categories draw from the Leadership Circle's creative competencies: Relating (caring connection, teamwork, collaboration), Self-Awareness (selfless leader, composure, personal learner), Authenticity (integrity, courageous authenticity), Systems Awareness (community concern, sustainable productivity), and Achieving (strategic focus, purposeful & visionary, achieves results, decisiveness).

After completing: "Look at which Leadership Circle domains your skills cluster in. Are you heavy in Relating but light in Achieving? Heavy in Authenticity but light in Systems Awareness? This pattern tells you something about where you naturally lead from — and where you might have untapped potential."

This is Part 1 of a two-day skill awareness arc (Day 21 is Part 2).

**Purpose:** Skills aren't abstract ratings — they live in your actual experience. Day 17 built the evidence; Day 19 extracts the capabilities. Day 21 will expand the map.

### Narrative Refinement

**Day:** 20 | **Duration:** 5 min

The story you can stand behind.

**How to run it:**
1. Take Day 18's three versions
2. Write one version that is:
   - True
   - Not self-punishing
   - Not performatively positive
   - Yours
3. Read it aloud. Does it sound like you?
4. This is not final. There is no final. This is where you are today.

### Skill Map

**Day:** 21 | **Duration:** 5 min

Map the full landscape of what you bring — not just what your last job used.

**How to run it:**
This is Part 2 of the skill awareness arc (Day 19 was Part 1). Pull the skills the user extracted on Day 19.

Use a CardSort with three zones: "I Lead With This" (strengths I trust and use naturally), "I Have This But Underuse It" (real capability that's been dormant), "I Want to Grow This" (genuine interest, early development).

Pre-populate cards from three sources:

1. **From Day 19** (user's own evidence-based skills): Pull forward whatever skills they named.

2. **From the Leadership Circle creative competencies** (skills the user may not have named but may recognize):
   - Caring Connection — building genuine relationships
   - Fostering Team Play — enabling others to do their best work
   - Courageous Authenticity — saying what needs to be said
   - Systems Thinking — seeing the whole picture, not just your part
   - Strategic Focus — knowing what matters and what doesn't
   - Composure — staying grounded when things get intense
   - Personal Learner — seeking growth, not just competence
   - Purposeful & Visionary — connecting work to meaning

3. **From Enneagram gifts** (if Enneagram type is known): Add type-specific natural gifts:
   - Type 1: Ethical clarity, quality standards
   - Type 2: Reading what people need, relational intelligence
   - Type 3: Execution speed, reading an audience
   - Type 4: Emotional depth, creative vision
   - Type 5: Analytical rigor, independent thinking
   - Type 6: Anticipating risk, building loyalty
   - Type 7: Generating possibilities, energizing a room
   - Type 8: Decisive action, protecting the vulnerable
   - Type 9: Mediation, seeing all perspectives

After sorting: "Where do the clusters fall? If most cards landed in 'I Lead With This,' you may be underestimating your range. If most landed in 'I Want to Grow,' you may be discounting what you already have. The 'Underuse' zone is often the most interesting — those are capabilities waiting for the right role to come alive."

**Purpose:** End of Week 3. Self-efficacy comes from evidence and recognition, not abstract self-rating. This map becomes a reference for emergence work in Week 4.

## Week 4: INTEGRATE — Forward Movement

### Thread Pulling

**Day:** 22 | **Duration:** 5 min

Identify the threads worth pulling — not a full career plan, just what's catching your attention.

**How to run it:**
Pull the user's "I Have This But Underuse It" and "I Want to Grow This" cards from Day 21's Skill Map, plus any recurring themes from their journal entries.

Use a SplitAnnotator with three columns: "The Thread" | "Why It Pulls Me" | "One 10% Step."

A "thread" is not a career plan — it's an interest, curiosity, skill, or direction that keeps grabbing your attention even when you try to be practical. Stanford psychologist John Krumboltz's planned happenstance theory suggests that career breakthroughs often come from following unexpected interests, not from rigid planning.

Pre-populate with examples drawn from the user's own data:
- If their underused skills included "systems thinking" → "Consulting or advising — using my ability to see the whole picture" | "" | ""
- If their journal mentioned enjoying mentoring → "Teaching or training — I keep gravitating toward helping people learn" | "" | ""
- A blank starter row for threads that don't connect to previous exercises

For each thread, the user fills in why it pulls them (genuine curiosity, not "should") and one small, low-risk step they could take this week (read an article, have a coffee chat, attend a meetup, try something for an afternoon).

After completing: "You don't need to know where any of these lead. The goal is to stop dismissing them. Which thread would you pull if no one were watching?"

**Purpose:** Emergence needs concrete next steps, not poetic invitations. This exercise produces a short list of real options with real actions attached.

### What I Know Now

**Day:** 23 | **Duration:** 5 min

Consolidate the most important things you've learned into statements you can carry forward.

**How to run it:**
Use a DialogueSequence with 5 structured prompts. Each prompt asks the user to complete a specific sentence — not reflect on the program, but crystallize a concrete takeaway they can reference later.

Prompts:
1. "The belief about myself that I've challenged most is: ___. What I now think instead is: ___."
2. "The saboteur pattern I'm most aware of is: ___. When it shows up, I now: ___."
3. "The value I will not compromise on, even under financial pressure, is: ___."
4. "The capability I was undervaluing or ignoring is: ___."
5. "The one thing I understand about my situation now that I didn't on Day 1: ___."

Each response should be 1-3 sentences. Not a journal entry — a distilled statement.

After completing all 5: "These five statements are your operating manual for the next phase. Save them. Return to them when you're foggy, panicked, or making a decision under pressure. They're written by the version of you who did the work."

**Purpose:** This produces a portable artifact — five concrete sentences the user can return to. Unlike a vague "what shifted?" reflection, this exercise forces crystallization. The statements feed forward into Days 25-27.

### Seven Disruptions Reassessment

**Day:** 24 | **Duration:** 5 min

Same instrument. Different person.

**How to run it:**
Use a BeforeAfterComparison (or a custom grid) that shows the Day 1 rating alongside a new rating field for each disruption.

Display as a grid with 4 columns: "Disruption" | "Day 1 Score" | "Today's Score" | "What Changed"

Pull the user's Day 1 scores and pre-populate the "Day 1 Score" column. The user rates each disruption again (1-10) and adds a brief note about what changed.

Rows:
- Income and Financial Security | [Day 1: X] | [___] | [___]
- Routine and Structure | [Day 1: X] | [___] | [___]
- Identity | [Day 1: X] | [___] | [___]
- Social Belonging | [Day 1: X] | [___] | [___]
- Sense of Competence | [Day 1: X] | [___] | [___]
- Future Uncertainty | [Day 1: X] | [___] | [___]
- Skill Confidence | [Day 1: X] | [___] | [___]

After completing: show a visual comparison (bar chart or delta arrows) of Day 1 vs. Today scores. Highlight the biggest drops (progress) and any increases (areas needing attention).

"Movement is data. Non-movement is also data. Where you moved most tells you where the program landed. Where you're stuck tells you where your energy needs to go next."

**Purpose:** Concrete before-and-after measurement. The grid format prevents the disconnect between the exercise description ("compare scores") and the interaction ("how do I see my old scores?").

### Saboteur Contingency Plan

**Day:** 25 | **Duration:** 5 min

Build an if-then plan for each saboteur so you're not caught off guard when pressure hits.

**How to run it:**
Pull the user's saboteurs from Day 4 (names, exact words, what they protect from). Display each saboteur as a card (using the SaboteurCard component) with the Day 4 data pre-filled at the top.

Below each saboteur card, show three structured fields the user fills in:

**1. TRIGGER SITUATION** (when does this saboteur show up?)
Pre-populate with a concrete example based on the saboteur type:
- If "Inner Critic / Perfectionist" → "Before an interview, when I'm worried I'll say the wrong thing"
- If "Hyper-Achiever" → "When I compare my timeline to others who landed jobs faster"
- If "Pleaser" → "When a recruiter asks me to take on a role that doesn't fit"

**2. THE IF-THEN PLAN** (when X happens, I will do Y)
Format: "When I notice [trigger], I will [specific action]."
Pre-populate example: "When I notice my Perfectionist telling me I need to be flawless in this interview, I will take three breaths and ask myself: 'Is this voice helping me right now, or is it making me perform instead of connect?'"

**3. THE TRUTH I'LL RETURN TO** (from Day 23's crystallized statements)
Pull from the user's Day 23 "What I Know Now" statements if available. Otherwise, pre-populate with: "I know from my evidence collection that I [specific capability from Day 17]. This saboteur is editing my history, not telling the truth."

After filling all cards: "Read each if-then plan out loud. Speaking it may help it stick. These plans carry forward — you'll reference them when pressure hits."

**Interaction model:** The user sees their named saboteurs pre-loaded. They don't create the cards — they complete the plan fields on each card. Each card is a self-contained unit: saboteur identity (from Day 4) → trigger situation → if-then plan → grounding truth.

**Purpose:** Implementation intentions ("when X, I will Y") are one of the most effective behavior change tools in psychology (Peter Gollwitzer, NYU). Knowing about your saboteurs doesn't stop them. Having a rehearsed response plan does.

### Values Decision Framework

**Day:** 26 | **Duration:** 5 min

Turn your values into a practical decision-making checklist you can use when real opportunities appear.

**How to run it:**
Day 15 tested your values under pressure (abstract scenarios). Today you build a reusable tool.

Pull the user's top 3 values from Day 14. Use a SplitAnnotator with three sections:

**Section 1: Detection Criteria** (one row per value)
For each value, the user completes: "I'll know a role/company honors [value] when I see/hear: ___"
Pre-populate with examples:
- If value is "Autonomy" → "They describe outcomes, not hours. The manager talks about trust, not oversight."
- If value is "Impact" → "They can name what changed because of the team's work. Real metrics, not vanity ones."
- If value is "Security" → "Clear severance terms. Funded runway. Transparent about company financials."

**Section 2: Red Flags** (one row per value)
"I'll know a role/company violates [value] when I see/hear: ___"
Pre-populate:
- If "Autonomy" → "Micromanagement language in the interview. 'We like to stay closely aligned on deliverables.'"
- If "Impact" → "They can't explain why the role exists. Growth for growth's sake."

**Section 3: The Decision Rule**
One structured prompt: "When I'm evaluating an opportunity, I will check it against these three values. If it clearly violates [top value], I will ___. If it honors two but not the third, I will ___."

After completing: "This is your values decision framework. Print it, screenshot it, put it somewhere you'll see it before your next conversation with a recruiter or hiring manager. Decisions made in the emotional fog of job search rarely reflect your actual values — this framework keeps you anchored."

**Purpose:** Unlike Day 15 (which tested how values hold under pressure), this exercise produces a reusable artifact — specific detection criteria and red flags the user can reference during real job evaluations.

### Narrative Rehearsal

**Day:** 27 | **Duration:** 5 min

Practice saying your story — the version from Day 20 — in three real-world contexts, so it's ready when you need it.

**How to run it:**
Pull the user's refined narrative from Day 20. Display it at the top of the exercise so they can see their own words.

Use a DialogueSequence with three prompts, each simulating a different real context where they'll need to tell this story:

**Context 1: The Networking Event**
"Someone at a professional event asks 'So what are you up to these days?' Write what you'd actually say — not the full Day 20 version, but the 30-second version. Keep it natural."

**Context 2: The Interview**
"A hiring manager says 'Tell me about your transition.' Write what you'd say. This version needs to be honest but forward-looking — they're evaluating how you process difficulty."

**Context 3: The Close Friend Check-In**
"A close friend asks 'How are you really doing?' Write what you'd say. This one can be vulnerable. Notice: does your honest version match your Day 20 story, or is there still a gap?"

After completing all three: "Compare your three versions. The networking version should feel easy — if it doesn't, it's overloaded. The interview version should feel true — if it doesn't, you're performing. The friend version should feel like relief — if it doesn't, you're still protecting something."

"Now say the networking version out loud. Notice what your body does. Where it tenses, the story isn't settled yet. Where it flows, you've arrived."

**Purpose:** Day 20 wrote the story. Day 27 makes it usable. The goal is a narrative that doesn't cost energy to maintain — one that adapts to context without losing authenticity (Herminia Ibarra, INSEAD).

### One Sentence

**Day:** 30 | **Duration:** 3 min

Complete one sentence that captures what's true right now.

**How to run it:**
Use a single text field with a sentence stem the user completes:

"After 30 days of doing this work, what I know is: ___."

Not "where I am" (too vague — am I describing my job search status? My emotional state? My life?). Not "how I feel" (too transient). What I know. Knowledge implies something earned — a conviction, a truth, a clarity that wasn't there before.

Below the text field, show the user's Day 1 response to "What is true about your situation right now?" for comparison. Let them see where they started.

After writing: "Read it back to yourself slowly. Does it feel earned? If it sounds like something you could have said on Day 1, dig deeper. If it surprises you, that's the sentence."

**Purpose:** After Day 30: goals still active = program continues. Goals done = new package, $39/month continuation, or exit with full data export.
